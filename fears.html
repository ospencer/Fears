Her fears of a Computer Science career.

"I'll be the minority."

Many girls worry about going into Computer Science because they are afraid of being the only girls in the workplace.

"According to CNET women actually make up about 30% of Computer Science. That is still a minority but it’s improving every year and before too many years may no longer be an issue. So if this is an issue for someone wanting to get into the field of Computer Science, remember that after four years of college there’s a good chance it may no longer be a problem.
Remember also that the only way to stay a minority in a field is to not join it because you’d be in the minority. If you are without fear and go for it, you can help end the minority and bring more gender equality to Computer Science."

—Nikolai Wotton, Northeastern University 2017

"I'll be harassed."

Harassment in the workplace comes in the form of unwelcome or offensive treatment from others based on things like race, sex, national origin, disability or age. Examples of harassment include pressure for dates or sexual favors, offensive or derogatory jokes, racial or ethnic slurs, cartoons or pictures, or unwelcome comments.

“Treatment of this nature in the workplace is not only mean, it is entirely unnecessary! In a work environment, there is a job that needs to be done. If someone is spending time going out of their way to harass their coworker, then they are clearly not focusing enough on the larger goals of the company. Also, people working together should feel like a team and encourage each other in all aspects of their lives. Supporting each other as individuals will result in the overall growth of their company. It’s like basketball, you need all team members working together in order to win the game!”

—Amanda Fode, Northeastern University 2017

"I won't be hired."

Some girls believe that they won’t be hired because they are a girl and all companies relating to Computer Science only want to hire boys.

"This is definitely the wrong mindset to have when thinking about getting into Computer Science. The first thing to remember is that this fear has no basis. Employers don’t care about your gender when hiring you any more than they care about your race, age, religion, or what you had for lunch today. Employers want employees that are good at what they do and that’s the extent of it. An example that demonstrates this is that recruiters at companies don’t ask for information like gender when determining whether to call a person in for an interview. They look at the content of the resume without knowing anything about the person other than what’s on the resume."

—Nikolai Wotton, Northeastern University 2017

"I'm not good enough."

Many people believe that software engineering is a man's job, programming is very logical, and women just aren't as logical as men.

"Here's the first thing that I will tell you: women are just as logical as men, and are just as capable as men at the thought processes that are required to be successful software engineers. There is no scientific evidence to suggest otherwise. Secondly, programming isn't actually as straightforward as one might think. It actually requires a substantial amount of creativity to come up with solutions to many very complex problems. It's definitely not always as simple as applying some known algorithm.

I have had the pleasure of working with some incredibly talented female engineers while working in the industry. I look to them for mentorship, and what they teach me is truly invaluable. The success of being an engineer is not a matter of gender, but rather how hard you are willing to work to be great at something you love."

—Oscar Lee Spencer III, Northeastern University 2017

"I won't be accepted."

One of the reason women decide not to go into computer science field is due to stereotypes. For example, the brogrammer culture is on the rise right now.

"Even though, companies like the one depicted in “Silicon Valley” do exist, they are not the only ones. There are plenty of businesses that take diversity very seriously and make everything they can to provide a welcoming atmosphere in order to attract talented people of different ages, genders and races. Such companies like Google, Microsoft and Apple constantly coming up with new idea to encourage young women to join tech world. There is also plenty of support from outside sources. My favorite example is Grace Hopper Celebration of Women in Computing(GHC). It is an annual conference that is attended by both women and men in order to promote diversity, provide networking opportunities, and offer chances to teach and learn. This year it is expected to grow to 15,000 attendees. I personally attended GHC of 2014 and 2015. I had an amazing time meeting various women in tech field and listening to the keynote speakers like Sheryl Sandberg(COO of Facebook) and Megan Smith(CTO of the United States)."

—Khrystyna Reyes, Northeastern University 2016
